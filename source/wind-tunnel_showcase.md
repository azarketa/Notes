(wind_tunnel_showcase_measurement_chain)=
## Wind-tunnel showcase

The generic uncertainty framework introduced so far is now specialized to the wind-tunnel campaign considered in the present document. The purpose of this section is not yet to develop the uncertainty tables themselves, but to describe the experimental protocol in terms that are directly compatible with them. In particular, the aim is to identify the measurement chain, the quantities that are directly read from the instrumentation, the quantities that are subsequently derived from those readings, and the way in which the recorded time series are converted into the representative values employed in the uncertainty budget.

(experimental_facility_and_model)=
### Experimental facility and model arrangement

The experiments are carried out in an open-circuit, low-turbulence wind tunnel driven by a 37 kW fan. The test section has a rectangular cross-section of $0.75\times1\ \mathrm{m}^2$ and a length of $3\ \mathrm{m}$, allowing freestream velocities of up to approximately $40\ \mathrm{m/s}$ while maintaining background turbulence levels below $0.2\%$. These characteristics make the facility suitable for steady aerodynamic measurements in the low-to-moderate Reynolds-number range.

The test body is a vertically mounted NACA0021 airfoil equipped with endplates, so that the experimental arrangement approximates a two-dimensional sectional configuration while preserving a well-defined geometric reference for force and wake measurements. The prototype consists of an extruded, spanwise-homogeneous model with chord:

$$
c=0.15\ \mathrm{m},
$$

and span:

$$
s=0.30\ \mathrm{m}.
$$

The corresponding aerodynamic reference area is therefore taken as:

$$
S_{\mathrm{ref}}=cs.
$$

These geometric quantities enter explicitly into the definition of the derived nondimensional coefficients and are therefore treated as basic measurands within the uncertainty budget. 

The angle of attack of the model is prescribed through a stepper-motor-driven rotary plate. Accordingly, the angle of attack $\alpha$ is treated as a basic measurand of the campaign, in the sense that it is imposed by the positioning system and subsequently used as an input variable in the reduction of the aerodynamic coefficients.

(measurement_chain_and_acquisition_protocol)=
### Measurement chain and acquisition protocol

The experimental campaign is conceived as a sequence of steady operating points. For each prescribed angle of attack, the tunnel is set to the target condition, the model incidence is adjusted through the rotary plate, the flow is allowed to stabilize, and the relevant channels are then recorded over a fixed acquisition window. In the present protocol, a stabilization time of $5\ \mathrm{s}$ is allowed after each angular adjustment, and the subsequent acquisition window has a duration of $60\ \mathrm{s}$ for all channels. The purpose of this procedure is to ensure that each operating point can be treated as a statistically steady configuration, so that the representative values of the measurands are given by the means of the corresponding time series.

All sensors are integrated into a National Instruments data-acquisition system and monitored through a custom LabVIEW interface. Different channels operate at different sampling frequencies according to the dynamic requirements of the measured variable. The force balance is sampled at $500\ \mathrm{Hz}$, the wake-pressure scanner at $750\ \mathrm{Hz}$, and the ambient and freestream probes at $100\ \mathrm{Hz}$. The acquisition duration is nevertheless common to all channels, so that each operating point is represented by a synchronous set of steady records of equal physical duration. The treatment of the resulting time series follows the statistical framework introduced earlier: the mean of each record provides the representative value of the corresponding quantity, the sample standard deviation characterizes its instantaneous scatter, and the uncertainty of the mean is obtained from the correlation-corrected standard error whenever temporal correlation is present. 

A separate drift protocol is employed for the force balance. Zero-load drift measurements are taken only at the beginning and at the end of the full campaign, each over a $10\ \mathrm{s}$ interval. The corresponding means are used to define a linearly varying zero reference in time, and the balance signal is corrected accordingly before computing the force-based aerodynamic coefficients. In the uncertainty budget, this procedure gives rise both to a correction of the force signal and to a residual drift-related uncertainty contribution associated with the assumed drift model.

(basic_measurands_of_the_campaign)=
### Basic measurands of the campaign

Within the present showcase, a **basic measurand** is understood as a quantity directly read from the instrumentation or directly prescribed by the test protocol. The basic measurands retained in the uncertainty tables are therefore the following.

The angle of attack $\alpha$ is a prescribed quantity, set by the stepper-motor-driven rotary plate. The chord $c$ and span $s$ are directly measured geometric quantities associated with the airfoil model and provide the reference scales entering the coefficient definitions. These quantities do not arise from time-series acquisition during the test itself, but they are still treated as basic inputs because they enter the derived aerodynamic quantities directly. Their associated uncertainty contributions are therefore purely bias-related in the sense of the Type B framework. 

The ambient thermodynamic state is monitored through a **Delta-Ohm HD2001.1** transmitter, which provides the three basic environmental measurands:
- ambient temperature $T$,
- barometric pressure $P$,
- and relative humidity $RH$.

These quantities are sampled continuously during each operating point and are later used to derive the air density $\rho$ and dynamic viscosity $\mu$ employed in the aerodynamic reductions. Since they are recorded as time series, they possess both a bias-related contribution, associated with the instrument specifications and calibration information, and a random contribution, associated with the scatter of the corresponding records. 

The freestream-velocity measurement chain employs three **Delta-Ohm HD49047T01L** Pitot-static probes. Probe 1 is placed on the inlet centerline of the test section and serves as a monitoring baseline for the incoming velocity. Probes 2 and 3 are attached to the upper endplate in the central region of the test section, laterally displaced on either side of the airfoil. Their purpose is to sense the local freestream condition in the reduced-cross-section region where the airfoil modifies the surrounding pressure field. Because probe 1 is located outside that reduced-cross-section region and therefore reads a systematically lower velocity level, it is not used in the final definition of the representative freestream velocity. Instead, the raw velocities from probes 2 and 3 provide the basis for the final reduced freestream quantity. In the uncertainty tables, the three raw probe outputs are denoted by:

$$
V_1^{\mathrm{raw}}, \qquad V_2^{\mathrm{raw}}, \qquad V_3^{\mathrm{raw}},
$$

and are treated as basic measurands. 

The aerodynamic force measurement retained in the present showcase is the lateral balance force $F_y$, measured by a **Kistler 9119AA2** piezoelectric balance. This force channel is the basic force measurand from which the lateral force coefficient and, subsequently, the lift coefficient are derived. Its uncertainty budget includes calibration, linearity, cross-talk, drift, and random time-series contributions. 

The drag-related basic measurands are the wake-rake dynamic-pressure readings $q_i$, with $i=1,\dots,64$, acquired through an in-house-designed wake rake connected to a **Chell microDaq3** 64-port pressure scanner. Each port delivers a direct pressure-related signal, and the set of 64 channels forms the basic wake survey from which the momentum-deficit drag coefficient is later computed. As with the other time-series-based measurands, each port signal contributes both a random term, obtained from the record itself, and a bias-related term associated with scanner calibration. The final drag coefficient is then obtained from the collective propagation of the 64 wake channels through the numerical integration scheme defined in the reduction model. 

(derived_measurands_and_data_reduction_chain)=
### Derived measurands and data-reduction chain

Once the basic measurands have been identified, the remaining quantities of interest are obtained through successive reduction steps. These quantities are treated as **derived measurands**, since they are defined through explicit functional relations involving one or more of the basic inputs.

The first layer of derived quantities is associated with the ambient thermodynamic state. The air density $\rho$ is obtained from the measured values of $T$, $P$, and $RH$ through the Picard/CIPM-2007{cite}`2007Picard` formulation, whereas the air viscosity $\mu$ is obtained from the same environmental inputs through the Zuckerwar-based model{cite}`1985Zuckerwar` implemented in the reduction script. In both cases, the derived quantity contains a bias-related part inherited from the uncertainty of the environmental channels and, additionally, an uncertainty contribution associated with the model itself whenever such an intrinsic equation-level term is considered in the budget. 

The second layer concerns the velocity reduction. Each raw probe signal is corrected through the temperature- and pressure-dependent full-scale factors employed in the instrumentation model, yielding the corrected velocities

$$
V_1,\qquad V_2,\qquad V_3.
$$

The representative freestream velocity of the operating point is then defined as the average of the two endplate probes:

$$
\overline{V}=\frac{V_2+V_3}{2}.
$$

This choice reflects the fact that probes 2 and 3 are located in the relevant central test-section region, whereas probe 1 is used only as a monitoring baseline of the incoming flow. The uncertainty associated with $\overline{V}$ therefore results from the propagation of the corrected uncertainties of probes 2 and 3. 

From the environmental quantities and the representative freestream velocity, the Reynolds number is then obtained as:

$$
Re=\frac{\rho\overline{V}c}{\mu}.
$$

This quantity is a derived measurand of purely algebraic nature, depending on the propagated uncertainties of $\rho$, $\overline{V}$, $c$, and $\mu$. It provides the natural bridge between the thermodynamic and kinematic reductions and the aerodynamic characterization of the operating point. 

On the force side, the corrected balance reading $F_y$ is reduced to the lateral force coefficient:

$$
c_y=\frac{F_y}{0.5\,\rho\,\overline{V}^{2}cs}.
$$

This coefficient uses the same environmental and freestream reductions discussed above, together with the geometric reference scales $c$ and $s$. Since the balance drift correction is applied before this reduction step, the uncertainty of $c_y$ must include not only the balance calibration and random terms, but also the propagated uncertainty associated with the adopted drift model. 

On the drag side, each wake-rake port is first interpreted as a local dynamic-pressure measurement $q_i$. From these portwise pressure values, the corresponding local wake velocities are reconstructed and inserted into the momentum-deficit formulation. The drag coefficient is then computed through the numerical integration of the wake integrand along the 64-port rake, using the Simpson-based quadrature rule defined in the table set. Thus, the drag coefficient $c_d$ is not derived from a single scalar input, but from the joint propagation of the 64 wake pressures together with the uncertainty contributions associated with $\rho$, $\overline{V}$, $c$, and the numerical integration procedure itself. 

Finally, the lift coefficient and aerodynamic efficiency are obtained from the previously reduced quantities. The lift coefficient is defined from the lateral-force and drag coefficients as:

$$
c_l=\frac{c_y-c_d\sin\alpha}{\cos\alpha},
$$

whereas the aerodynamic efficiency is defined as:

$$
E=\frac{c_l}{c_d}.
$$

These quantities therefore occupy the final level of the reduction hierarchy. Their uncertainty budgets are not based on new direct measurements, but on the propagated uncertainties of the preceding derived measurands and, in the case of $c_l$, on the prescribed angle of attack.

(uncertainty_budgets)=
## Uncertainty budgets

The description above provides the measurement-level interpretation of the variables appearing in the uncertainty tables. The basic measurands are those directly prescribed or read from the instrumentation: $\alpha$, $c$, $s$, $T$, $RH$, $P$, $V_i^{\mathrm{raw}}$, $F_y$, and $q_i$. The derived measurands are those obtained from them through the reduction chain: $\rho$, $\mu$, $V_i$, $\overline{V}$, $Re$, $c_y$, $c_d$, $c_l$, and $E$. Their order is not arbitrary, but reflects the physical structure of the campaign: environmental quantities are first reduced, then the representative freestream condition is built, then the force and wake measurements are nondimensionalized, and finally the lift, drag, and efficiency measures are obtained from those intermediate quantities.

The uncertainty budgets that follow should therefore be read as the quantitative counterpart of the present descriptive section. They do not introduce new variables; rather, they formalize how the measurement chain described here is converted into a propagated uncertainty budget, with the Type A and Type B contributions attached to each basic and derived measurand according to the framework established in the previous sections.
